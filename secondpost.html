How income inequality can be seen in poverty.

Explaining income inequality with the poverty line and distribution of wealth statistics.

Posted by Grace Hallett on April 28, 2023

Poverty is one devastating effect of income inequality that is often misunderstood. First, to understand the income gap in the United States, the nation’s family incomes have been ranked from lowest to highest and then dividing into fifths. This tactic allows us to see how income is divided between each fifth.

“The poorest fifth enjoys only 3.8% of the nation’s income, while the richest fifth enjoys 53.2%. Another way of saying this is that the richest 20% of the population have as much income as the remaining 80% of the population.”

Statistics also do not account for the true scope of poverty in the United States. The national calculation to determine the rate of poverty in the United States was developed in 1963 by Mollie Orshansky and is called the poverty line. Although adjusted annually for inflation, this calculation does not take into account for changes in society. Since the 1960s, families spend increasingly more on things such as energy, child care, and health care. Therefore, the poverty line that we see today is likely inacurate and does not account for everyone experiencing poverty.

This graph shows the distribution of wealth between each fifth of the nation.

As you can see, the highest quintile of the nation holds over 50% of the nation's wealth, while each lower quintile holds significantly less. This is a prime example of income inequality.
